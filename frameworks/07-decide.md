# Stage 7 — Decide (with the Simplicity Filter)

> **Goal:** Turn everything the pipeline learned into a **build / pivot / kill**
> call — and force it through a *simplicity filter* so the answer is something a
> smart non-expert (your parents) would understand and believe. **Gate:** You can
> explain, in plain language, what it is, why anyone would pay instead of using
> what already exists, and why it's worth your years.

Humans have short attention spans, and complexity hides weakness. An idea that
can only be justified in jargon usually can't be justified. The final output is
not a 30-page report — it's a clear answer to a few human questions. If it can't
pass the simplicity filter, that is itself a finding.

## The Simplicity Filter — explain it to your parents

Imagine explaining the business to a parent who is *not* in the industry but
understands value, purpose, money, and cost. Write answers in their language —
no jargon, no acronyms, no "platform / synergy / leverage." Five questions:

1. **What is it, in one breath?** One sentence a 12-year-old could repeat. If it
   takes a paragraph, you don't understand it yet.
2. **Who is it for, and what problem do they have?** A real person and a real
   pain (straight from the [problem brief](01-problem.md)).
3. **Why would they pay for this instead of what they already do / could just
   buy?** *This is the load-bearing question.* Name the existing alternative and
   why it's not good enough. "There's nothing like it" is almost always wrong and
   means you haven't found the alternative. If the honest answer is "they'd
   probably just use X," the business doesn't clear the filter.
4. **How does it make money, and does the money work?** Where the cash comes from,
   roughly what it costs to deliver, and why there's margin — in euros, not
   metaphors. (Pulled from [Simulate](05-simulate.md).)
5. **Why is this worth doing — and why you?** The purpose/impact, and the unfair
   reason this founder wins it (from the [profile](00-profile.md)).

If all five answer cleanly and a non-expert nods, the idea has clarity. Murkiness
on any one — especially #3 — is a red flag the earlier stages may have
rationalized past.

## The decision

State one of:
- **BUILD** — the problem is real, the idea cleared audit/simulate/test, the
  economics work, and it passes the simplicity filter. Give the first milestone,
  the budget, and the next assumption to de-risk.
- **PIVOT** — the problem is real but the current solution/shape is wrong (e.g.
  "operate first, build later," or a different customer). Keep what's validated,
  change what failed, state the new hypothesis.
- **KILL** — a fatal risk with no plausible mitigation, the economics can't clear
  the gate, or it fails the simplicity filter (especially "why not just buy X?").
  A kill is a success: you saved years.

Tie the decision to the **kill criteria written back in the [Audit](04-audit.md)**
— were they hit?

## Output: the decision memo (`07-decision-memo.md`) + the pitch (`pitch.md`)

- **Decision memo** — what you believed, what you tested, what you measured, the
  call, and the reasoning. The investor's-eye summary.
- **Pitch** — the simplicity-filter answers, written for a non-expert. This is the
  artifact you actually share. If you can't write a crisp `pitch.md`, you are not
  ready to decide.

## Discipline

- **Lead with the verdict and the one-breath description**, then support it. Bury
  nothing important.
- **The "why not just buy X" answer is the whole game.** Most ideas die on it, and
  they should die on paper, here, for free.
- **Round honestly and cut jargon.** False precision and complexity are how weak
  ideas survive review. The simplicity filter exists to stop that.
