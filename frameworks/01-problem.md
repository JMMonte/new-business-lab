# Stage 1 — Problem

> **Goal:** Understand the *problem* deeply enough to know whether it's worth
> solving at all — **before** anyone falls in love with a solution. **Gate:** You
> can state whose problem it is, what it costs them today, why it isn't already
> solved, and what evidence would prove it *isn't* worth solving.

This is the stage the lab learned it was missing. Almost every idea that wastes
months does it by solving a problem nobody will pay to have solved — a solution in
search of a problem. Solutions are seductive and easy to generate; problems are
hard and boring to understand. So we force the problem first. **If you arrived
with a solution already (you usually will), park it — and interrogate the problem
it claims to solve as if the solution didn't exist.**

## The 6 questions (the Problem Crucible)

A problem is worth solving only if it survives all six. Answer each with
specifics and a confidence tag (`[measured]/[benchmarked]/[guess]`).

1. **Whose problem, exactly?** Name one specific person/segment — a role, not
   "businesses" or "people." If you can't picture one real person who has it this
   week, you don't have a problem yet.
2. **What is the pain, concretely?** Describe the exact moment it hurts. Then rate:
   - **Frequency** — how often does that moment happen? (daily / monthly / yearly)
   - **Intensity** — how much does it hurt when it does? (annoyance → existential)
   - A problem that is rare *and* mild is not a business. You want frequent ×
     intense, or rare × severe-and-expensive.
3. **What do they do about it today?** The status quo is your real competitor —
   usually a spreadsheet, a person, a workaround, or *nothing*. Describe it
   honestly. "Nothing" often means the pain isn't big enough to act on.
4. **What does the problem cost them today?** Put a number on it — money, hours,
   risk, lost revenue. **If you can't quantify the cost, willingness-to-pay is a
   fantasy.** The price you can charge is bounded by this number.
5. **Why hasn't it been solved already?** This is the question that kills most
   ideas — and reveals the rare good ones. Exactly one of these is true:
   - **(a) It just became solvable/urgent** — a real *why-now* (new tech, new
     regulation, a cost curve crossing, a behavior shift). This is the green light.
   - **(b) It's genuinely hard** and you have an unfair reason to crack it now.
   - **(c) It's been tried and failed** — find the graveyard and learn why.
   - **(d) It isn't actually worth solving** — smart people looked and walked away.
     This is the default, and the trap. Assume (d) until you can prove (a)/(b)/(c).
6. **Why not just buy/use what already exists?** State the closest existing
   alternative and *why it's not good enough for this person*. If "use X" is a fine
   answer, there is no business. (This question returns at the end — the
   [Decide](07-decide.md) simplicity filter makes it the headline.)

## Revealed preference beats stated pain

What people *do* about a problem is worth ten times what they *say*. Before
believing a problem, look for evidence people already act on it:

- They **pay** for a workaround, a consultant, or a bad incumbent.
- They **built** an internal hack / spreadsheet / Zapier mess to cope.
- They **switched** or **churned** in frustration.
- They complain about it **unprompted** in communities and reviews.

No revealed-preference signal anywhere = treat the problem as `[guess]` and make
finding that signal the first job of [Research](research.md) / [Test](06-test.md).
(See `source-valuation.md` Axis 2.)

## Output: the problem brief (`problem-brief.md`)

Short. One page. The six answers, each tagged; the status-quo alternative; the
quantified cost; the why-now; and a one-line **falsifier**: the finding that would
make a rational person say "this problem isn't worth solving." If you can't write
the falsifier, you're not being honest yet.

## Gate check

Advance to [Ideate](02-ideate.md) only if: the customer is one nameable person,
the pain is frequent×intense or rare×expensive, the cost is quantified, the
why-now is real (not "AI is big now"), and "just buy X" has a real answer. A
problem that fails here doesn't need a better solution — it needs to be dropped,
and that's a win (you saved the next six stages).

## Discipline

- **Don't smuggle the solution into the problem.** "People need *my-app*" is not a
  problem. "Solo landlords lose ~6 hrs/month reconciling tips for tax and fear an
  audit" is.
- **Beware the founder's-own-itch trap.** Your itch is a great *source* of
  problems and a terrible *proof* of one. You are an N of 1; find the other N.
- **Problems are durable; solutions are disposable.** A well-understood problem
  outlives every pivot. Spend here.
